Interdisciplinary Thinker

Being open to unfamiliar experiences and learning from feedback constantly teaches me about new ways to problem solve, organize, and understand different points of view. I get many questions asking how public health/anthropology, human-centered design, and business are related in any capacity.

My answer is always the same: While these subjects do not seem related at first glance, there are applicable skills and knowledge from each subject that can assist my work in another.

I find that my interdisciplinary approach to issues has helped cultivate a unique approach to problem-solving, by facilitating open discussions between different groups of people. To me, understanding how groups work within a company - their goals, their strategies, their capabilities, and limitations - is essential for efficient and cohesive goal completion.

T-Shaped Designer

My broad educational experience has allowed me to become what Tim Brown, CEO of IDEO, calls a T-shaped designer. My background in anthropology, human-computer interaction and business allows me to approach design prompts and issues from many different angles and communicate with developers, researchers, and designers alike to better understand their needs.

My specialization in anthropology gives me a deep understanding of human behavior, culture, and social dynamics, which helps me cultivate thorough and endearing results in user research and testing. This, along with my experience in science writing and biology lab research, allows me to take dense or complicated findings and transform them into usable information for design solutions.

Human-Centered Enthusiast

My experience in business strategy taught me many valuable lessons about strategic marketing and product management/design. To me, the value of creating a relatable and endearing experience for users and consumers is of utmost importance when it comes to success.

By combining my experience in anthropology with business I find that many strategic solutions can be found by understanding a customer base and exploring factors that determine how they use, buy, and perceive products.

My experience in anthropology also assists me in making design decisions. Throughout my college education, I have performed school-related and personal ethnographies, teaching me how to ask the right questions and how to gain a deeper understanding of why people behave the way they do.

I find that these experiences have greatly assisted me in UX research, as user testing, persona creating, and journey mapping often hinge on our perception of users and the data we collect from them.
