Take a look

Overview

This was a volunteer project for a local start-up called IceBrekr in Nashville. Icebrekr is a networking application, like a mix of LinkedIn and Instagram. The company came to Nashville Entrepreneur Center with their observations from beta testing.

The Problem

Beta test users often stopped the sign-up process very early on, and those who complete the sign-up process fail to edit the profiles or show any further activity on the app.

Root Cause

The beta testing showed that users were put off by the complicated sign-in process and confusion about the app's purpose once they logged in, leaving users unsure about how to answer certain questions and complete their profile.

The Goal

The goal of this redesign is to create a more informative and cohesive user experience, therefore increasing the number of users that will sign up and complete their profiles. The application needs to have more explicit guidance and information aout the app.

The Solution

I redesigned IceBrekr to provide a better experience for new users by focusing on educating users about the app, and organizing/streamlining the onboarding process.

Analyzing IceBrekr's Usability Test Result

If users aren't completing the Sign-in/Profile process, where are they stopping?

Users often stopped when interacting with LinkedIn's login button and the "Fill From LinkedIn Profile" button.
Additionally, when it came to the profile page many users stopped editing their profile after adding a picture.

The red circles indicate the most frequent final touchpoint of each page before the user quits.

Why are users stopping at these prompts?

The whole process is too confusing. Even the slightest deferral of attention or unclear step that gets in the way can intimidate users into giving up or getting distracted. Minimizing confusion for the users is essential since IceBrekr's substitutes (Like LinkedIn or Facebook) are the prevalent competitors in this space.

The cognitive processing cost of signing up outweighs the personal motivation to subscribe to yet another networking app.

"I keep getting sent back to the original login page after using the LinkedIn button but none of my information was showing up. I kind of just gave up after that, like thats just way too much work."

"After I got to the word picking part I was so confused because like I don't even know what this app is for. How am I supposed to know what words to pick?"

By the numbers:

The data shows that people are definetly discouraged by confusing prompts and language, as well as a complicated sign-up process (mainly that the LinkedIn button simply did not work). Almost 50% of the users that didn't complete the sign-up processes claimed their confusion caused the deferral. This tells me that confusion is a major pain point for most users, and I should create some sort of quick tutorial.

Personas

After discussing the intended users with the CEO and doing some informed brainstorming about social media and networking apps, I created some personas to guide my design intentions. What I found is that the primary audience is business professionals looking for jobs or recruiters looking to hire.

Other groups that should be considered are people entering a new industry, people looking for personal work (babysitting, personal assistant), or people looking to find like-minded people/hobby groups. I created three personas (an early careerist, a recruiter, and a teacher looking for a hobby group) to assist me in creating a great experience for every user.

Journey Map

To me, making journey maps for redesigns is really helpful. By doing this I can do a Gap Analysis, which is basically a comparison of the current user flow and the ideal user journey to determine the primary changes that would complete the final goal.

This is the rough sketch journey map I drew up during the meeting at Nashville Entrepreneur Center.

Insights and Brainstorming

After completing the research, I reviewed the data and responses as I brainstormed insights. Based on the research the primary insights for this specific project are:

  1. Users want more direction

    Users want a simple and direct tutorial on how to use IceBrekr before having to answer questions about thier profile.

  2. IceBrekr's target audiences are job candidates and recruiters looking to fill positions

    While this is a networking app for professional and informal occasions, most users are using IceBrekr in expand their professional network - lots of candidates and recruiters (very wide range of people).

  3. IceBrekr's competitive advantage is its speed, and the application must reflect that

    Processes and functions need to be quick. The draw of IceBrekr is that its a location-based real-time variation of LinkedIn. This means that everything from profile creation to posting needs to be able to happen as quickly as possible.
